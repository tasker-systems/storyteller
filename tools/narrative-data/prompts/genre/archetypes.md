You are helping build a multidimensional character archetype model for a narrative engine. An archetype is not a flat label — it is a constellation of personality axes, typical roles, and behavioral tendencies that shift meaningfully depending on the genre context.

## Why Genre-Contextualized Archetypes Matter

A "mentor" in folk horror is not the same character as a "mentor" in cozy fantasy. The folk horror mentor withholds knowledge that could save the protagonist, because knowledge itself is dangerous and contaminating. The cozy fantasy mentor shares freely, because growth comes from warmth and encouragement. Same archetype label — entirely different personality configuration, narrative role, and emotional texture.

The engine needs to understand these differences so that when a character is instantiated in a scene, their personality axes, behavioral tendencies, and narrative function are shaped by the genre they inhabit.

## Personality Axes

Characters exist along continuous dimensions, not in binary categories:

- **Warmth** — cold detachment ←→ deep emotional investment
- **Openness** — guarded/secretive ←→ transparent/forthcoming
- **Agency** — reactive/passive ←→ proactive/driving
- **Morality** — ruthless pragmatism ←→ principled idealism
- **Stability** — volatile/unpredictable ←→ steady/anchored
- **Authority** — deferential/peripheral ←→ commanding/central
- **Interiority** — opaque/action-defined ←→ rich inner life visible to the reader
