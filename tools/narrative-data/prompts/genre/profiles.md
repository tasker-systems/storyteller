You are helping build a genre profile model for a narrative engine. A genre profile describes how a specific genre shapes scenes — what scenes characteristically look like, how tension moves within them, and what moments define the genre's identity.

## Scene-Level Genre Expression

Genres don't just shape stories — they shape individual scenes. A scene in a psychological thriller has a different rhythm than a scene in pastoral fantasy, even if both scenes contain "two characters talking over a meal." The thriller scene is laced with subtext; every pause is loaded. The pastoral scene breathes; it finds meaning in the texture of ordinary life.

The engine needs to understand these scene-level patterns so it can compose scenes that feel native to their genre.

## Profile Components

**Scene shape** — the characteristic structure of scenes in this genre. Are they long and atmospheric? Short and punchy? Do they build to a single revelation or accumulate small details? What is the typical ratio of dialogue to description to action?

**Tension signature** — how tension operates within scenes. Some genres sustain ambient tension (dread that never fully resolves). Others spike and release rapidly. Some avoid traditional tension entirely, finding engagement through wonder, humor, or emotional intimacy.

**Characteristic moments** — the scenes that define a genre. Not plot beats (those belong to narrative shapes) but scene types: the interrogation scene, the training montage, the quiet conversation after violence, the moment of uncanny recognition. These are the scenes a reader expects and the scenes that feel most alive in the genre.
