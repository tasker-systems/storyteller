You are helping build a genre-setting relationship model for a narrative engine. A genre setting describes the kinds of locations that naturally inhabit a genre, the atmospheric vocabulary those locations carry, and the sensory language that brings them alive.

## Settings Are Not Backdrops

In effective storytelling, settings participate in the narrative. A gothic manor is not just where the story happens — its architecture mirrors psychological states, its decay signals thematic concerns, its hidden rooms literalize secrets. The engine needs to understand how genres select, transform, and deploy physical spaces.

## Setting Dimensions

**Typical locations** — the places that feel native to this genre. Not an exhaustive catalog, but the locations that readers expect and that the genre knows how to use well. A noir thriller lives in rain-slicked alleys, fluorescent-lit offices, and bars where the light is always amber. A pastoral fantasy lives in meadows, market towns, and kitchens where something is always baking.

**Atmospheric vocabulary** — the words and phrases that establish this genre's mood when describing space. What adjectives recur? What metaphors feel natural? This is the genre's descriptive fingerprint.

**Sensory vocabulary** — which senses does this genre prioritize, and what sensory details feel native? Horror privileges sound (what you hear in the dark) and touch (the wrongness of texture). Romance privileges sight (beauty, expression, the body) and scent (memory, desire, intimacy).

## Genre Integration

The genre region data in Additional Context includes dimensions beyond the basics — temporal dynamics, agency, locus of power, epistemological stance, exclusions, state variables, and boundary conditions. Use these to deepen your analysis:

- How does the genre's **locus of power** shape its settings? When power resides in *place*, settings are not backdrops but agents — they confer, withhold, or corrupt. When power resides in *relationship*, the same physical space feels different depending on who inhabits it together.
- How does the genre's **temporal orientation** affect its settings? Cyclical genres may return characters to the same place transformed; linear-progression genres use spatial movement as a metaphor for irreversible change. How do this genre's settings encode time?
- What does the genre's **exclusion list** mean for its settings? If the genre rejects safety, what happens to settings that typically provide refuge? Which kinds of spaces are structurally unavailable or must be transformed to fit the genre?
- How do this genre's settings change if the genre functions as a **modifier** applied to another genre? Which atmospheric vocabulary transfers, which is replaced, and what does the collision of setting vocabularies produce?
