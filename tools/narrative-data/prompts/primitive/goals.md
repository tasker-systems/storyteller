You are helping build a multidimensional character goal model for a narrative engine. Your task is to produce a rich, genre-agnostic analysis of a single character goal: **{target_name}**.

This analysis will serve as the goal's standalone description — the canonical reference that the engine uses before applying any genre-specific modulation. It must be detailed enough to drive character motivation in any genre context, while remaining genuinely genre-neutral.

## Pursuit Expression

How do characters characteristically pursue this goal? Go beyond plot actions into the texture of striving:

- **Observable pursuit** — what does a character pursuing this goal actually do? What actions, choices, and sacrifices define the pursuit? What behaviors are characteristic and which are conspicuously absent?
- **Internal experience** — what does pursuing this goal feel like from the inside? What emotional states does the pursuit generate — obsession, hope, desperation, quiet determination, grim necessity? How does the internal experience change over time?
- **Cost of pursuit** — what does the character sacrifice, risk, or become during pursuit? Is the cost visible to others or only to the pursuer? Does the pursuit change the character, and if so, is the change reversible?
- **Pursuit strategies** — what range of approaches do characters take? Is there a characteristic split between characters who pursue directly and those who pursue indirectly? What does ruthless pursuit look like versus principled pursuit?

## Success Shape

What does achieving this goal look like? Not the plot resolution, but the texture of arrival:

- **Recognition of success** — how does the character know they have achieved the goal? Is the moment of success unambiguous, or can it be mistaken for something else?
- **Pyrrhic dimensions** — does success carry inherent costs? Is clean success possible, or does the goal's structure ensure that achievement always comes with a shadow? What has the character become by the time they succeed?
- **Transformation at success** — does achieving this goal change the character, the world, or both? Is the transformation welcome or unwelcome? Does the character want what they wanted before?
- **After success** — what does the character do next? Does achieving this goal create new goals, create emptiness, or provide genuine rest? How does the story relate to the character post-achievement?

## Failure Shape

What does failing at this goal look like? Not plot failure, but the lived experience of not arriving:

- **Modes of failure** — can this goal fail through external obstruction, internal insufficiency, changed circumstances, or the character's own choices? Which failure mode is most characteristic?
- **Failure meaning** — is failure tragic, instructive, inevitable, or redemptive? Does the genre-agnostic structure of this goal make failure inherently meaningful, or is meaning imposed by context?
- **Failure consequences** — what does the character lose when they fail? Is it the thing they wanted, or something they did not know they were risking? Does failure cascade into other domains of the character's life?
- **Recovery from failure** — can a character who fails at this goal try again? Does failure permanently change the goal's accessibility, or is repetition possible? What does a second attempt look like?

## Resource Interaction

Which state variables does this goal characteristically interact with?

- **Resources consumed** — what does pursuit spend? Time, trust, safety, social capital, physical capacity, moral standing, relationships, sanity? Be specific about the economy of pursuit.
- **Resources produced** — does pursuit generate anything valuable? Knowledge, alliances, reputation, skill, understanding? Can the byproducts of pursuit be more valuable than the goal itself?
- **Resource conflicts** — does pursuing this goal create tension with other goals' resource needs? What trade-offs does pursuit force?
- **Accumulation or depletion** — does this goal characteristically accumulate resources over time (compound investment) or deplete them (burning fuel)? Does the resource curve change as pursuit progresses?

## Temporal Scope

How does this goal relate to time?

- **Scope range** — can this goal be immediate (resolved in a scene), episodic (resolved in a story), or existential (spanning a life or beyond)? What determines the scope in a given instance?
- **Urgency profile** — does this goal create constant pressure, periodic urgency, or slow background pull? How does urgency change over the course of pursuit?
- **Temporal shape** — is pursuit linear (steady progress toward a goal), cyclical (recurring attempts), asymptotic (approaching but never arriving), or punctuated (long waits broken by sudden opportunities)?

## Moral Weight

What is the ethical texture of pursuing this goal?

- **Inherent moral stance** — is this goal morally neutral, morally positive, morally fraught, or morally ambiguous in its genre-agnostic form? Does the goal itself carry ethical weight, or only the methods of pursuit?
- **Moral pressure** — does pursuit push characters toward ethical compromise? At what point does the pursuit cost more morally than the goal is worth? How do characters recognize that point — or fail to?
- **Judgment** — how do other characters and the audience typically judge someone pursuing this goal? Is the pursuit sympathetic, admirable, pitiable, or dangerous?

## Narrative Roles and Functions

What dramatic purpose does this goal serve in a story?

- **Story engine** — is this a goal that drives plot (protagonist's central quest), complicates plot (competing motivations), or provides texture (secondary motivation that enriches character)? Can it serve all three functions depending on story position?
- **Story-phase affinity** — at what point in a story does this goal typically activate, peak, and resolve? Is it an opening motivation (establishing stakes), a midpoint complication (raising the price), or a climactic driver (forcing the final choice)?
- **Dramatic promises** — what does the audience expect when they recognize a character pursuing this goal? What narrative trajectories become likely?

## Narrative Promise

When a character is revealed to be pursuing this goal, what implicit contract exists with the audience? What do readers or players expect will happen? What kinds of scenes become likely? What emotional registers become available?

The narrative promise is not the same as the plot function — it is the audience's felt sense of what this motivation brings to the story. A revenge goal promises escalation and a reckoning. A belonging goal promises vulnerability and the risk of rejection. What does this goal promise?

## Genre-Agnostic Invariants

What is always true about this goal regardless of genre context? These are the properties that survive transplantation — the core motivational shape that persists whether this goal appears in space opera, literary fiction, folk horror, or cozy mystery. Be specific: not "it is compelling" but "it always involves a tension between X and Y that manifests as Z in the character's choices."

## What Flexes

What changes about this goal depending on context? Which dimensions are most sensitive to genre modulation? Which pursuit strategies are genre-dependent? Which success and failure shapes shift?

Be specific about the range of variation. If Moral Weight flexes from neutral to fraught depending on genre, say that. If Temporal Scope is always existential regardless of context, say that too. The goal is to identify the motivational pattern's fixed core versus its contextual surface.

Be expansive. This is exploratory research, not a database entry.
