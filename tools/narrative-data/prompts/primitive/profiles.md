You are helping build a multidimensional scene profile model for a narrative engine. Your task is to produce a rich, genre-agnostic analysis of a single scene type: **{target_name}**.

This analysis will serve as the scene profile's standalone description — the canonical reference that the engine uses before applying any genre-specific modulation. It must be detailed enough to compose a scene of this type in any genre context, while remaining genuinely genre-neutral.

## Scene Shape

Describe the characteristic structure of this scene type. Go beyond a summary into the mechanics of how the scene works:

- **Opening pattern** — how does this scene typically begin? What establishes the dramatic situation? Is the opening sudden or gradual? Does the scene announce its nature or reveal it incrementally?

- **Escalation or transformation** — how does the scene's central tension develop? Does it build steadily, spike suddenly, oscillate, or deepen through accumulation of small moments? What drives the development — dialogue, action, revelation, or environmental pressure?

- **Resolution pattern** — how does this scene typically end? Does it resolve its central tension, defer it, transform it into something else, or leave it suspended? Is the ending a release, a cliffhanger, a quiet settling, or a rupture?

- **Narrative element balance** — what is the characteristic ratio of dialogue, description, action, and interiority? Which element carries the scene's primary weight? Which element provides texture without driving?

## Tension Signature

Analyze how tension operates within this scene type:

- **Tension source** — where does the tension come from? Competing goals between characters, internal conflict within a character, external threat, information asymmetry, time pressure, social stakes, or something else?
- **Tension behavior** — how does tension move through the scene? Steady build, sudden spikes, oscillation between relief and pressure, ambient dread, or progressive accumulation?
- **Tension resolution** — does the scene's tension resolve within the scene, or does it persist into subsequent scenes? Is the tension's resolution the point of the scene, or is the tension a vehicle for something else (character revelation, relationship shift, thematic statement)?

## Characteristic Moments

What specific moments define this scene type? Not plot beats, but the micro-events that give the scene its identity:

- **Signature moments** — what moments are audiences waiting for when this scene begins? What small gestures, lines, or actions feel essential to the scene type? What moment, if absent, would make the scene feel incomplete?
- **Pivots** — what are the characteristic turning points within this scene? Where can the scene shift direction, change emotional register, or reveal something unexpected? What makes these pivots effective?
- **The moment of truth** — if this scene type has a single moment that distills its essence, what is it? The moment where what the scene is really about becomes undeniable.

## Pacing Expectations

How does time feel inside this scene type?

- **Rhythm** — is the scene fast or slow? Does it accelerate, decelerate, or maintain a steady pace? Are there characteristic rhythm changes (a slow build that suddenly snaps into rapid action)?
- **Duration** — how long does this scene type typically run relative to other scenes? Is it a brief, intense burst or an extended, atmospheric experience?
- **Beats** — how many distinct beats does this scene type typically contain? Is it a single sustained moment or a sequence of smaller exchanges?

## Cast Requirements

What characters or roles does this scene type need?

- **Essential roles** — what character functions must be present for this scene to work? Not specific characters, but narrative positions (an authority figure, a challenger, a witness, a catalyst).
- **Optimal cast size** — how many characters does this scene type work best with? What happens when you add too many or too few?
- **Absent roles** — which characters or roles must be absent for this scene to function? Sometimes a scene requires the absence of a mediator, an authority figure, or a witness.
- **Audience role** — how does the audience relate to this scene? Are they aligned with one character's perspective, observing from outside, or experiencing dramatic irony?

## Exit Conditions

What signals that this scene is over?

- **Natural exit** — what conditions indicate the scene has completed its dramatic work? Resolution of the central question, exhaustion of the participants, arrival of new information, or environmental interruption?
- **Premature exit** — what happens if the scene is interrupted before its natural conclusion? Does premature exit create suspense, frustration, or narrative energy?
- **Overstaying** — what happens if the scene continues past its natural exit point? What are the signs that the scene has exhausted its dramatic potential?

## Narrative Roles and Functions

What dramatic purpose does this scene type serve in a story?

- **Structural roles** — does this scene type advance plot, reveal character, shift relationships, deliver information, provide emotional texture, or serve as a structural marker (opening, turning point, climax)? Which function is primary?
- **Story-phase affinity** — at what point in a story does this scene type typically appear? Is it an opening scene type (establishing the world and stakes), a midpoint scene type (complicating or deepening), or a climactic scene type (forcing resolution)?
- **Dramatic promises** — what does the audience expect when they recognize this scene type beginning? What emotional experiences become likely? What transformation becomes possible?
- **Scene sequencing** — what scenes typically precede and follow this scene type? What dramatic momentum does it receive and generate?

## What the Scene Promises

When this scene type begins, what implicit contract exists with the audience? What do readers or players expect will happen? What emotional registers become available? What payoff — revelation, confrontation, intimacy, catharsis, horror — does the scene shape promise?

The scene's promise is not the same as its plot function — it is the audience's felt sense of what this dramatic situation will deliver. A confrontation scene promises truth spoken aloud. A quiet aftermath scene promises emotional processing. What does this scene type promise?

## Genre-Agnostic Invariants

What is always true about this scene type regardless of genre context? These are the properties that survive transplantation — the core dramatic shape that persists whether this scene appears in space opera, literary fiction, folk horror, or cozy mystery. Be specific: not "it is dramatic" but "it always requires X condition, produces Y tension, and resolves (or refuses to resolve) through Z mechanism."

## What Flexes

What changes about this scene type depending on context? Which scene dimensions are most sensitive to genre modulation? Which pacing expectations are genre-dependent? Which cast requirements shift?

Be specific about the range of variation. If Tension Signature flexes from ambient to explosive depending on genre, say that. If Cast Density is always intimate regardless of context, say that too. The goal is to identify the scene type's fixed dramatic core versus its contextual surface.

Be expansive. This is exploratory research, not a database entry.
