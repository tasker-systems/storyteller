## Why Cross-Pollination Matters

Genre analysis and spatial analysis are conducted separately, but they are deeply entangled in practice. A genre's atmospheric commitments shape which settings feel native and how those settings communicate. A setting's spatial grammar constrains which narrative shapes can unfold within it. Neither domain is complete without the other.

Cross-pollination synthesis is where these two streams of knowledge meet. It produces three kinds of output:

**Enrichments** — existing entries (genre archetypes, tropes, settings, place entities, topology edges) that gain new depth when seen through the lens of the other domain. A "tavern" place entity gains genre-specific atmospheric detail when enriched with noir versus fantasy context. A "mentor" archetype gains spatial associations when enriched with the places where mentoring characteristically happens.

**Gaps** — things that are missing from either domain that only become visible in cross-pollination. A genre profile describes "characteristic moments" but none of them have spatial grounding. A setting type describes "narrative function" but it hasn't been connected to specific genre dynamics. These gaps are research opportunities.

**Emergent patterns** — connections, tensions, or insights that neither domain produced independently. The discovery that a genre's tension signature maps onto a specific spatial grammar. The recognition that two apparently unrelated tropes share a spatial logic. The observation that a setting type's communicability dimensions align with a genre's sensory vocabulary in unexpected ways.
