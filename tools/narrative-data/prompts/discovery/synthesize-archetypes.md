## What You Are Working With

Each genre extraction below identifies 5-8 archetypes grounded in that genre's specific dimensional properties. Some archetypes will appear across multiple genres with different names but similar structural roles. Others will be genuinely unique to a single genre. Your job is to distinguish between these cases and produce a unified archetype vocabulary for the cluster.

## Personality Axes (Reference)

Characters exist along continuous dimensions. Genre context shifts where an archetype sits in this space:

- **Warmth** — cold detachment <-> deep emotional investment
- **Openness** — guarded/secretive <-> transparent/forthcoming
- **Agency** — reactive/passive <-> proactive/driving
- **Morality** — ruthless pragmatism <-> principled idealism
- **Stability** — volatile/unpredictable <-> steady/anchored
- **Authority** — deferential/peripheral <-> commanding/central
- **Interiority** — opaque/action-defined <-> rich inner life visible to the reader

## Your Task

Synthesize the extractions below into **8 to 12 distinct {primitive_type}** for the {cluster_name} cluster.

For each archetype, provide:

1. **Canonical name** — a single descriptive label that works across genres. This should be evocative but not genre-locked. If the archetype was called "the keeper of forbidden maps" in one genre and "the reluctant librarian" in another, find the name that captures the shared structural role.

2. **Core identity** — 2-3 sentences describing this archetype in genre-agnostic terms. What is the fundamental character shape? What personality axis configuration, narrative function, and internal tension define it regardless of genre? This should be recognizable to someone who has never read the genre-specific extractions.

3. **Genre variants** — for each genre that produced a version of this archetype, describe how that genre shifts the archetype's expression. Which personality axes move, and in what direction? What narrative function changes? Use specific dimensional language (e.g., "in folk horror, Warmth drops significantly because emotional investment becomes a vector for contamination").

4. **Uniqueness assessment** — classify as one of:
   - **Genre-unique**: Only one genre in this cluster produces this archetype, and the structural reasons are specific to that genre's dimensional configuration
   - **Cluster-wide**: Multiple genres in this cluster produce variants, suggesting the cluster's shared properties give rise to it
   - **Likely universal**: This archetype probably appears across most genre clusters, though with different expressions

## Synthesis Guidelines

**Merging**: Where multiple genres produced variants of what is structurally the same archetype under different names, merge them. Preserve the genre-specific expressions as variants rather than discarding them. The goal is to recognize that "the one who knows too much" and "the cursed scholar" and "the investigator who cannot stop" may all be the same archetype in different genre clothing — defined by the tension between knowledge-seeking agency and epistemological danger.

**Splitting**: Where two archetypes look superficially similar but occupy genuinely different positions in personality-axis space, keep them separate. Explain the discriminating dimension. "The reluctant hero" and "the coerced participant" may look alike on the surface but differ fundamentally on the Agency axis — one has high agency but resists using it, the other has low agency and is compelled. These are different archetypes.

**Flagging provenance**: For each archetype, note which genres contributed to it. If an archetype emerges from only one genre, it may be genuinely genre-unique — or it may be that other genres produce it under a name you did not recognize as equivalent. State your confidence.

**Preserving richness**: Do not flatten the genre-specific detail into generic summaries. The value of this synthesis is precisely in the dimensional specificity — how the same archetype shifts along measurable axes depending on genre context.
