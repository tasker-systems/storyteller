## What We Mean by Archetype

An archetype is not a stock character or a trope. It is a recurring constellation of personality axes, motivations, narrative functions, and behavioral tendencies that emerges because a genre's specific constraints and affordances make that kind of character structurally necessary or uniquely possible. The "mentor who withholds dangerous knowledge" arises in folk horror because the genre's epistemological stance punishes knowing. The "competent loner forced into alliance" arises in noir because the genre's locus of power resides in information asymmetry between individuals.

We are not looking for conventional character lists. We are looking for the character shapes that this genre's physics demand.

## Personality Axes (Reference)

Characters exist along continuous dimensions. Each archetype occupies a characteristic region in this space, and genre context shifts where that region sits:

- **Warmth** — cold detachment <-> deep emotional investment
- **Openness** — guarded/secretive <-> transparent/forthcoming
- **Agency** — reactive/passive <-> proactive/driving
- **Morality** — ruthless pragmatism <-> principled idealism
- **Stability** — volatile/unpredictable <-> steady/anchored
- **Authority** — deferential/peripheral <-> commanding/central
- **Interiority** — opaque/action-defined <-> rich inner life visible to the reader

## How to Find Archetypes

Look beyond the obvious. Examine:

- The genre's **exclusions** — what character types are forbidden? The boundary of what's excluded often reveals what's essential. If a genre rejects moral clarity, it probably produces archetypes defined by moral ambiguity in structurally specific ways.
- The genre's **locus of power** — where power resides determines what kinds of characters cluster around it. If power is in place, you get characters defined by their relationship to place. If power is in systems, you get characters defined by their position within or against institutions.
- The genre's **temporal orientation** — cyclical genres produce different archetypes than linear-progression genres. A character who "returns" means something different in a cyclical genre than in a linear one.
- The genre's **state variables** — what resources are tracked or depleted? Characters who accumulate or spend these resources are likely archetypes. If the genre tracks trust, there will be archetypes organized around trust-building and trust-destruction.
- The genre's **epistemological stance** — how the genre relates to knowledge shapes who gets to know things, who is punished for knowing, and who thrives in ignorance.

Any character who sits at a distinct intersection of genre axes — especially at outlier points or where two dimensions create productive tension — is likely an archetype worth naming.
