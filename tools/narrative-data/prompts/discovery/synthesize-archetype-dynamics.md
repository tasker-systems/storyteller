1. **Canonical pairing name** — the best label for this dramatic relationship pattern across the cluster
2. **Core tension** — 2-3 sentences describing the dramatic energy this pairing produces genre-independently. What structural incompatibility or dangerous complementarity drives it?
3. **Scale** — orbital, arc, scene, or cross-scale? Note if the same pairing operates at different scales in different genres
4. **Genre variants** — how does this pairing express differently across the cluster's genres? Note:

   - Whether the pairing's resolution differs (one genre allows reconciliation, another forbids it)
   - Whether nonhuman agents participate in some genre variants
5. **The signature scene** — what is the characteristic dramatic situation this pairing produces? Describe it at the cluster level — the scene shape that recurs because this kind of tension demands this kind of confrontation
6. **Relational graph properties** — what kind of edge does this pairing produce? Asymmetric or reciprocal? What currencies flow along it? Does the edge constrain other relationships?
7. **Uniqueness** — cluster-specific, genre-unique, or likely universal?

## Merging and Splitting Guidelines

- **Merge** when two genres produce the same dramatic tension between structurally equivalent archetypes. The Warden/Vessel pairing in folk horror and the Guardian/Outsider pairing in cosmic horror may be the same structural tension (custodian enforcing a system's demands on an unwilling subject) even though the archetypes have different names.
- **Keep separate** when two pairings involve the same *roles* but different *tensions*. A mentor-student pairing driven by knowledge-withholding is structurally different from one driven by succession-anxiety, even if both involve a more-powerful and less-powerful archetype.
- **Keep separate** when two pairings operate at different scales. An orbital enmity between archetype families is a different dramatic resource than a scene-level rivalry between individual characters.
- **Flag** pairings where the tension is genre-defining — where removing this pairing would collapse the genre's dramatic engine.
- **Flag** pairings that cross the human/nonhuman boundary — character-entity dynamics that arose in the archetype pairings data.
