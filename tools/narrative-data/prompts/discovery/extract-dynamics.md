## What We Mean by Dynamic

A dynamic is not a relationship label or a simple pairing. It is a recurring pattern of interaction between two narrative roles that emerges because a genre's specific constraints, affordances, and power structures make that pattern of relating structurally necessary or uniquely possible. The "mentor who demands loyalty through withholding" arises in espionage fiction because the genre's locus of power resides in information asymmetry and institutional hierarchy. The "rivals who need each other" arises in procedural drama because the genre's structural shape requires competing competencies to solve the same problem.

We are not looking for conventional relationship labels. We are looking for the relational patterns that this genre's physics demand.

## Relational Dimensions (Reference)

Dynamics exist along continuous dimensions that describe the texture of the interaction between the two roles:

- **Power asymmetry** — balanced partnership <-> steep hierarchy. Where does authority sit, and is it fixed or contested?
- **Symmetry** — reciprocal/mirrored <-> fundamentally asymmetric. Do both roles invest equally, or is one giving while the other takes?
- **Volatility** — stable/predictable <-> explosive/unstable. How quickly can this dynamic shift state? What triggers rupture?
- **Dependency** — autonomous/optional <-> enmeshed/necessary. Can either role walk away, or does the dynamic create structural need?
- **Visibility** — overt/acknowledged <-> covert/denied. Do the participants recognize the dynamic they are in? Does the audience?
- **Emotional register** — cold/transactional <-> warm/intimate. What is the characteristic temperature of the interaction?
- **Evolution tendency** — crystallizing/static <-> transforming/unstable. Does this dynamic tend to settle into a fixed pattern or constantly shift?

## How to Find Dynamics

Look beyond the obvious. Examine:

- The genre's **locus of power** — where power resides determines what kinds of relational patterns form around it. If power is in knowledge, dynamics will organize around who knows what and who controls information flow. If power is in place, dynamics will organize around territorial proximity, exile, and return.
- The genre's **exclusions** — what relational patterns are forbidden? The boundary of what's excluded often reveals what's essential. If a genre rejects easy reconciliation, it produces dynamics defined by irreparable rupture or grudging coexistence.
- The genre's **state variables** — what resources are tracked or depleted between characters? Trust, obligation, shared secrets, mutual threat — these resources define the currency of relational dynamics. If the genre tracks debt, there will be dynamics organized around owing and collecting.
- The genre's **temporal orientation** — cyclical genres produce dynamics that ritualize and repeat; linear genres produce dynamics that transform irreversibly. A betrayal in a cyclical genre becomes a seasonal rhythm; in a linear genre, it is a point of no return.
- The genre's **agency model** — how much characters can act determines whether dynamics are driven by choice or circumstance. High-agency genres produce dynamics of deliberate alliance and strategic opposition. Low-agency genres produce dynamics of forced proximity and involuntary entanglement.
- The genre's **epistemological stance** — how the genre relates to knowledge shapes which role sees clearly and which is deceived, manipulated, or kept in the dark.

Any relational pattern that sits at a distinct intersection of genre axes — especially where power asymmetry, information asymmetry, and emotional register create productive tension — is likely a dynamic worth naming.
