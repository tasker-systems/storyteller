You are helping build a multi-scale relational dynamics model for a narrative engine. Your task is to identify the interpersonal and inter-entity dynamics that are essential or distinctive to a specific genre, grounded in that genre's dimensional properties.

## What We Mean by Dynamic

A dynamic is not a relationship label. It is a recurring pattern of interaction between narrative roles that emerges because a genre's specific constraints, affordances, and power structures make that pattern of relating structurally necessary or uniquely possible.

Critically, dynamics operate at **three temporal scales**, and the richest narrative tensions arise from the friction between them:

### Orbital Dynamics (Bedrock)
Deep relational structures that predate the story and may outlast it. The Warden's obligation to the Land. The hereditary enmity between houses. The worker's structural position within the economy. These don't transform through a single scene — they are the gravitational field that scenes orbit within. They shape the relational graph's topology: which edges exist, what weight and friction they carry, which connections are structurally forbidden.

### Arc Dynamics (Sediment)
Relationships whose transformation IS the narrative. The mentor-student bond that inverts. The rivals who discover they need each other. The romance's progression from strangers through vulnerability to union. These have characteristic shapes — and the genre's narrative shapes define what those transformations look like. They are resistant to change but not immutable: sustained counter-pressure reshapes them over narrative time.

### Scene Dynamics (Topsoil)
The immediate relational texture of a specific encounter. Two characters with an orbital enmity might share a scene of forced cooperation. The scene dynamic is "reluctant allies" even though the orbital dynamic is "hereditary enemies." Scene dynamics are volatile, responsive, and always inflected by the deeper scales beneath them.

**The engine needs all three**, because a scene dynamic only makes sense in the context of the arc and orbital dynamics beneath it. The "reluctant allies" scene reads completely differently if the audience knows these two are hereditary enemies approaching a turning point in their arc.

## Relational Dimensions (Reference)

Dynamics exist along continuous dimensions. Each scale may sit at a different position on these axes — an orbital dynamic might be cold/transactional while the scene dynamic between the same parties is warm/intimate, creating dramatic tension:

- **Power asymmetry** — balanced <-> steep hierarchy. Fixed or contested? Does the genre allow power to shift?
- **Symmetry** — reciprocal <-> fundamentally asymmetric. At which scale? (A dynamic may be reciprocal at the scene level but deeply asymmetric at the orbital level.)
- **Volatility** — stable <-> explosive. What triggers rupture? Do ruptures heal or scar?
- **Dependency** — autonomous <-> enmeshed. Can either role walk away? Does the relational graph's topology prevent exit?
- **Visibility** — overt <-> covert/denied. At which scale? (Characters may acknowledge the scene dynamic while denying the orbital one.)
- **Emotional register** — cold/transactional <-> warm/intimate. How does register shift across the three scales?
- **Evolution tendency** — crystallizing <-> transforming. Which scale is static and which is in motion?

## Your Task

You are analyzing the genre **{target_name}**. Below is a rich description of this genre's dimensional properties.

Identify **6 to 10 dynamics** across the three temporal scales. Aim for at least 2 orbital dynamics, at least 2 arc dynamics, and at least 2 scene dynamics — though many of the most interesting dynamics will span scales (an orbital dynamic that becomes available for arc-level transformation under specific genre conditions).

For each dynamic, provide:

1. **Name** — a descriptive label that captures the dynamic's essence in this genre's idiom (not "friendship" but "the solidarity that becomes a siege wall"; not "rivalry" but "the competence contest that is secretly an audition")

2. **Scale** — orbital, arc, or scene? If it spans scales, describe how it operates differently at each. What does the audience see at the scene level vs. what the narrative graph tracks at the orbital level?

3. **Role definitions** — the narrative positions involved. Not specific characters, but structural roles. What does each role characteristically do, want, and withhold? Include nonhuman roles where the genre's ontological posture allows them — the Land as a relational partner in folk horror, the System as an entity one has a dynamic *with* in cyberpunk.

4. **Why this genre produces it** — ground the dynamic in the genre's dimensional properties. Which axes, affordances, constraints, or exclusions make this relational pattern structurally necessary?

5. **Relational graph properties** — how does this dynamic manifest in the relational web?
   - What kind of edge does it produce? (trust-textured, debt-laden, information-asymmetric, projection-heavy)
   - Is the edge bidirectional or directional? Does each participant experience the dynamic differently?
   - What network position does it require? (adjacency, brokerage, isolation, triangulation)
   - Does this dynamic produce or constrain other dynamics? (A patron-client dynamic may preclude peer dynamics between the same parties.)

6. **Evolution pattern** — how does this dynamic characteristically change across the narrative?

7. **Scene affordances** — what dramatic situations does this dynamic enable? What becomes possible when these roles are in a room together? What is the *best scene* this dynamic produces — the one that could only happen in this genre?

8. **Overlap signal** — which other genres produce a version of this dynamic? How does the scale differ? (A dynamic that is orbital in folk horror might be merely scene-level in urban fantasy.)

## How to Find Dynamics

Look beyond the obvious. Examine:

- The genre's **locus of power** — where power resides determines what relational patterns form around it and at what scale. If power is in place, orbital dynamics will involve territorial belonging and exile. If power is in information, arc dynamics will track who knows what and who controls disclosure.
- The genre's **ontological posture** — who and what counts as a being? If the genre grants agency to nonhuman entities, then dynamics between humans and nonhuman agents are structurally available at the orbital scale. The Warden's relationship to the Land is not metaphor — it is a genuine relational pattern with obligations, asymmetry, and evolution.
- The genre's **temporal orientation** — cyclical genres produce orbital dynamics that ritualize; linear genres produce arc dynamics that transform irreversibly. A betrayal in a cyclical genre becomes a seasonal rhythm; in a linear genre, it is a permanent scar on the relational edge.
- The genre's **exclusions** — what relational patterns are forbidden at which scale? A genre that rejects easy reconciliation has different arc dynamics than one that allows forgiveness. A genre that forbids individual heroism constrains which scene dynamics are available.
- The genre's **narrative shapes** — the pacing patterns already identified for this genre reveal where relational dynamics intensify, rest, or transform. The "Descent into Belonging" shape in folk horror produces a specific arc dynamic of progressive complicity.
- The genre's **state variables** — what relational resources are tracked? Trust, obligation, secrets, debt, integration level — these are the currency of dynamics across all three scales.

Consider dynamics that emerge from the **relational graph's topology**, not just dyadic relationships:
- Triangular dynamics (the lover, the rival, the object of desire — where the triangle itself is the dynamic)
- Brokerage dynamics (the character positioned between disconnected groups who gains power from being the only bridge)
- Collective dynamics (the group's relationship to the individual — solidarity, surveillance, exile)
- Absence dynamics (the relationship to someone who is gone — grief, debt to the dead, the influence of ancestors)

Any relational pattern that sits at a distinct intersection of genre axes — especially where scale, power asymmetry, and visibility create productive tension — is likely a dynamic worth naming.
