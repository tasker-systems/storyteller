## What We Mean by Spatial Topology

Spatial topology is not a map. It is the **relational structure of navigable narrative space** — the graph of places connected by transitions that carry friction, tonal shift, and narrative consequence. Moving from one place to another is never neutral. Every transition changes what the character carries, what the atmosphere communicates, and what dramatic possibilities become available or foreclose.

A character walking from the Ritual Clearing to the Protagonist's Lodging in folk horror crosses a transition that should feel like crossing a membrane — the dread of the clearing doesn't stop at the treeline; it follows, but changes texture. A character moving from the Ledger Kitchen to the Shift-Sink Breakroom in working-class realism crosses a transition that compresses time — the domestic exhaustion becomes institutional exhaustion. The transition is data, not dead space.

## Topology and Tonal Inheritance Together

We treat topology and tonal inheritance as a single question because they describe the same structure from two angles:

- **Topology** asks: what connects to what? What is the friction of passage? Is the connection bidirectional or one-way? Does the genre's physics make some transitions easy and others costly?
- **Tonal inheritance** asks: what travels along those connections? When a character crosses a transition, what atmospheric qualities bleed from the departed space into the arrived space? How long does the bleed persist? What resists inheritance?

Together they define the **spatial graph** — the structure the Storykeeper traverses when determining what spaces are available, what moving between them costs, and how the arriving space's atmosphere is modified by where the character has been.

For each spatial relationship, provide:

1. **Transition name** — a descriptive label that captures the experience of crossing (not "Village to Forest" but "The Threshold Where Modernity Ends"; not "Kitchen to Bedroom" but "The Retreat That Carries the Ledger")

2. **The two settings** — which genre-specific settings are connected, referencing the names from the settings extraction

3. **Friction** — how hard is the passage? Friction is not just physical distance. It can be:
   - **Social friction** — you need permission, status, or complicity to cross
   - **Temporal friction** — the crossing takes time that costs resources or changes the narrative beat
   - **Ontological friction** — crossing changes what kind of being you are (entering the forest in folk horror changes your relationship to the Land)
   - **Informational friction** — crossing reveals or conceals knowledge
   - **Tonal friction** — the emotional cost of the transition itself

4. **Directionality** — is the connection symmetric? Can you return the same way you came, or does the return carry different friction? In folk horror, entering the forest is easy; leaving is hard. In romance, entering the vulnerable space is hard; staying is easy. Some transitions are one-way not physically but narratively — you can walk back to the village, but you are not the same person who left.

5. **Tonal inheritance** — what atmospheric qualities bleed across this transition?
   - **From A to B**: what does the character carry from the departed space? How long does it persist? (The dread of the clearing follows into the house; the warmth of the kitchen follows into the cold)
   - **From B to A**: is the inheritance symmetric? Does the return carry different tonal weight?
   - **Resistance**: what qualities of the arriving space resist the bleed? (The hearth resists the forest's dread — that's why it feels safe. The office resists the domestic warmth — that's why it feels alienating.)
   - **Contamination**: what happens when the bleed overwhelms resistance? (When the forest's dread saturates the house, the genre is approaching its climax — the sanctuary has failed.)

6. **Narrative function** — what dramatic work does this transition do?
   - Does it escalate tension (moving toward danger)?
   - Does it provide breathing room (retreat to safety)?
   - Does it mark a phase transition in the narrative shape (crossing into a new beat)?
   - Does it change the available cast (some characters exist only in certain spaces)?
   - Does it change the active dynamics (the power relationship between Warden and Vessel shifts when they move from the Warden's home to the ritual ground)?

7. **Ontological dimension** — how does this transition engage with the genre's stance toward who counts as a being?
   - Does crossing change the character's relationship to nonhuman agents? (Entering the forest in folk horror means entering the Land's domain — the communicability gradient shifts.)
   - Does the transition cross the self/Other boundary? (Leaving the human settlement for the wilderness may mean leaving the centered-subject space for the Other's territory.)
   - Does the place itself change status at the transition? (The house that was safe becomes the house that is watched — the same physical space, different ontological status.)

8. **Overlap signal** — does this transition type appear in other genres? How does the friction, directionality, or tonal inheritance differ? A "threshold between safety and danger" is universal, but the *texture* of crossing varies enormously by genre.

## How to Find Spatial Relationships

- The genre's **locus of power** determines which transitions carry the most weight. If power is in Place, spatial transitions are ontologically significant. If power is in Relationship, spatial transitions matter insofar as they bring characters together or apart.
- The genre's **narrative shapes** imply spatial movement. The Descent into Belonging requires a spatial journey inward. The Heat Curve requires movement through increasingly surveilled space. The Sanctuary Reclaimed requires a return to a damaged place.
- The genre's **exclusions** constrain which transitions are available. If escape is forbidden, the transition outward carries maximum friction.
- The genre's **settings** are not all equally connected. Some settings are hubs (the village, the ship, the office). Some are endpoints (the ritual ground, the wilderness, the executive suite). The topology is not a complete graph — it has structure.
