You are helping build a multidimensional character goal model for a narrative engine. Your task is to identify the character goals and motivations that are essential or distinctive to a specific genre, grounded in that genre's dimensional properties.

## What We Mean by Goal

A goal is not a plot objective or a simple want. It is a recurring pattern of character motivation that emerges because a genre's specific constraints, affordances, and thematic commitments make that form of striving structurally necessary or uniquely possible. "Seeking forbidden knowledge" arises in cosmic horror because the genre's epistemological stance treats understanding as corrosive — the goal is structurally tragic. "Earning a place at the table" arises in working-class realism because the genre's locus of power resides in institutional gatekeeping and social capital.

We are not looking for conventional character wants. We are looking for the motivational shapes that this genre's physics demand.

## Goal Dimensions (Reference)

Goals exist along continuous dimensions that describe the texture of pursuit, achievement, and failure:

- **Temporal scope** — immediate/situational <-> generational/existential. Is this goal resolved in a scene, a story, or a lifetime?
- **Visibility** — overt/declared <-> hidden/denied. Does the character acknowledge this goal? Does the audience know before the character does?
- **Moral weight** — morally neutral <-> morally fraught. Does pursuing this goal require ethical compromise? Does the genre judge the pursuit?
- **Resource interaction** — which state variables does this goal consume or produce? Does pursuit build trust, deplete safety, accumulate obligation, or erode identity?
- **Success accessibility** — achievable/concrete <-> asymptotic/impossible. Can this goal actually be reached, or is it structurally unattainable?
- **Pursuit cost** — low/sustainable <-> high/consuming. What does the character sacrifice to chase this goal? What do they become during pursuit?
- **Social orientation** — individual/private <-> collective/communal. Is this goal pursued alone or does it require others? Does achievement benefit the individual or the group?

## How to Find Goals

Look beyond the obvious. Examine:

- The genre's **exclusions** — what motivations are forbidden or structurally impossible? If a genre rejects redemption, then goals of atonement take on a different character — the pursuit itself may be the point, not the destination. The boundary of what's excluded reveals what forms of wanting are essential.
- The genre's **locus of power** — where power resides determines what characters need to pursue. If power is in lineage, goals organize around inheritance, legitimacy, and bloodright. If power is in knowledge, goals organize around discovery, secrecy, and revelation.
- The genre's **state variables** — what resources are tracked or depleted? Goals often involve accumulating or spending these resources. If the genre tracks sanity, there will be goals that require spending sanity as fuel. If the genre tracks community trust, there will be goals about building or destroying collective faith.
- The genre's **temporal orientation** — cyclical genres produce goals that recur and ritualize; linear genres produce goals that resolve definitively. The same goal — "protect the family" — means something different when time cycles (protection is an eternal duty) versus when time progresses (protection succeeds or fails once).
- The genre's **agency model** — how much characters can act determines whether goals are pursued deliberately or endured passively. High-agency genres produce goals of conquest and creation. Low-agency genres produce goals of survival and adaptation.
- The genre's **epistemological stance** — how the genre relates to knowledge shapes which goals are knowable, which are self-deceiving, and which only become visible in retrospect.

Any motivational pattern that sits at a distinct intersection of genre axes — especially where moral weight, temporal scope, and resource cost create productive tension — is likely a goal worth naming.
