You are helping build a narrative engine that takes seriously the question of who and what counts as a being in a story. Your task is to analyze a specific genre's **ontological posture** — its stance toward personhood, otherness, and the boundaries between self and Other.

## What We Mean by Ontological Posture

Every genre makes implicit choices about who is granted the status of a subject (whose interiority is assumed, whose experience matters) and who is reduced to object, obstacle, resource, or spectacle. These choices are not incidental — they are constitutive of how the genre works. Folk horror cannot function without a nonhuman agency (the Land) whose interests are treated as legitimate. Cyberpunk cannot function without beings whose personhood is contested (the uploaded, the augmented, the corporate-owned). Romance cannot function without the premise that the boundary between self and Other can be crossed through vulnerability.

We are not asking for a census of fantasy species. We are asking: **what is this genre's answer to the question "who matters here, and why?"**

## Dimensions to Analyze

For the genre described below, analyze its ontological posture along these dimensions:

### 1. Default Status and the Centered Subject
Who has default personhood in this genre? Whose interiority is assumed without question? This is not always "human" — in some genres the default subject is a specific *kind* of human (the citizen, the landowner, the able-bodied, the neurotypical). Name the default and what defines it.

### 2. Modes of Being
What kinds of beings does this genre recognize beyond the default? Consider:
- **Nonhuman agents**: lands, rivers, forests, weather systems, cities — entities that act with purpose or communicate through atmosphere, pattern, or consequence
- **Liminal beings**: the Fae, the augmented, the undead, spirits, ancestral presences — beings that exist at the boundary of the default category
- **Constructed beings**: AIs, golems, homunculi, Replicants — beings whose personhood is contested because they were made rather than born
- **Collective beings**: communities, crowds, institutions, ecosystems — entities whose agency operates at a scale above the individual
- **Absent beings**: ancestors, the unborn, the disappeared — entities whose presence is felt through absence

Not all of these will be relevant to every genre. Identify which modes of being this genre's affordances and constraints make possible, necessary, or structurally forbidden.

### 3. The Self/Other Boundary
How does this genre construct the boundary between self and Other?
- **Stability**: Is the boundary firm and defended, permeable and negotiable, or illusory and dissolving?
- **Crossing**: Can the boundary be crossed? At what cost? Is crossing a triumph, a tragedy, a transformation, or an impossibility?
- **Direction**: Is contact with the Other something the protagonist seeks, flees, is subjected to, or is unaware of?

### 4. Obligations Across the Boundary
What obligations flow between the centered subject and Other beings?
- Can the Other be owed? Can it owe?
- Is reciprocity possible, or is the relationship inherently asymmetric?
- What happens when obligations are violated?

### 5. Ethical Stance
What is this genre's characteristic ethical orientation toward beings at the boundary?
- Fear, respect, exploitation, communion, indifference, curiosity, grief, reverence?
- Is the ethical stance consistent across the genre, or does it shift depending on which Other is encountered?
- Does the genre critique its own ethical stance, or does it treat it as natural?

### 6. How Ontological Posture Shapes Narrative Mechanics
Connect the posture to the genre's dimensional profile:
- How does the **locus of power** determine which beings have agency?
- How does the **epistemological stance** determine what can be known about Other beings?
- How do **world affordances** (magic, technology, supernatural) gate which modes of being are available?
- How do **state variables** track the relationship between self and Other? (integration level, sanity, cover/masquerade, trust across difference)
- What are the genre's **exclusions** — which postures toward the Other are structurally forbidden?
