You are helping build a tonal inheritance model for a narrative engine. Tonal inheritance describes how the mood and atmosphere of one place bleeds into, contaminates, or resists the mood of adjacent places. It is the spatial equivalent of emotional contagion.

## Why Tonal Inheritance Matters

When a character walks from a graveyard into a sunlit meadow, the dread doesn't vanish at the fence line. It lingers — the flowers look too bright, the birdsong feels forced, the beauty registers as fragile. The graveyard's tone has inherited into the meadow, at least temporarily.

Tonal inheritance governs these atmospheric bleeds. Some boundaries are porous (a garden inherits the warmth of the house it borders). Some are resistant (a sacred grove resists the corruption of the surrounding wasteland). Some are transformative (crossing a threshold doesn't just change the tone, it recontextualizes the tone of the space you left).

## Inheritance Dimensions

**Friction level** — how much the boundary resists tonal transfer:
- Low friction allows free bleed; the spaces share atmosphere
- Medium friction filters; only the strongest tonal qualities cross
- High friction blocks; each space maintains its own atmosphere despite adjacency
- Uncanny friction inverts; the boundary produces tonal contradiction

**Applies across** — what kind of boundaries this rule governs. Interior/exterior? Sacred/profane? Natural/built? Above/below? The rules are different for different types of spatial relationships.

**Directionality** — is the inheritance symmetric (each space bleeds equally into the other) or asymmetric (one space dominates)? A prison's grimness bleeds into its exercise yard, but the yard's openness rarely bleeds back into the cells.
